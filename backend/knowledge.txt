HR ATTRITION ANALYSIS — COMPREHENSIVE KNOWLEDGE BASE
======================================================

1. PROJECT OVERVIEW
======================================================

This is an AI-powered HR analytics platform that analyzes, predicts, and provides insights on employee attrition using the IBM HR Analytics Employee Attrition dataset. The system integrates Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA), Machine Learning (Gradient Boosting with SMOTE), and Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) to deliver actionable HR intelligence.

The platform has three core modules:
1. Analysis Dashboard — Interactive visualizations revealing attrition patterns across departments, roles, satisfaction levels, compensation, and tenure.
2. Prediction Engine — Real-time ML-based attrition risk scoring for individual employees.
3. AI Chat Assistant — Natural language Q&A powered by RAG architecture using FAISS vector search and Groq LLM.

Technology Stack:
- Frontend: Streamlit (Python)
- Backend: FastAPI (Python)
- ML Model: Gradient Boosting Classifier (scikit-learn) with SMOTE for class balancing
- RAG Pipeline: LangChain + FAISS + HuggingFace Embeddings (all-MiniLM-L6-v2) + Groq LLM (Llama 3.1 8B Instant)
- Dataset: IBM HR Analytics Employee Attrition Dataset from Kaggle

3. EXPLORATORY DATA ANALYSIS INSIGHTS
======================================================

Attrition by Demographics:

Age:
- Employees aged 18-25 show the highest attrition rate at approximately 35%.
- Attrition decreases steadily with age.
- Employees above 45 show very low attrition below 10%.
- Young employees may leave due to career exploration and lack of organizational attachment.

Gender:
- Male attrition rate: approximately 17%.
- Female attrition rate: approximately 15%.
- Gender difference is marginal and not a primary driver.

Marital Status:
- Single employees show the highest attrition rate at approximately 25%.
- Married employees: approximately 13% attrition.
- Divorced employees: approximately 10% attrition.
- Single employees may have fewer personal commitments anchoring them.

Attrition by Compensation:

Monthly Income:
- Employees earning below $3,000 per month show attrition rates exceeding 25%.
- Employees earning above $8,000 per month show attrition rates below 10%.
- Income is one of the strongest predictors of attrition.
- The income distribution is right-skewed, meaning most employees earn below the mean.

Stock Options:
- Employees with Stock Option Level 0 (no options): highest attrition at approximately 25%.
- Employees with Stock Option Level 1: approximately 12% attrition.
- Employees with Stock Option Level 2-3: below 8% attrition.
- Stock option level is the number one most important feature in the prediction model.

Salary Hike:
- Employees receiving salary hikes below 13% tend to have higher attrition.
- Higher salary hikes correlate with lower attrition but the effect is moderate compared to base income.

Attrition by Job Factors:

Overtime:
- Employees working overtime: approximately 30% attrition rate.
- Employees NOT working overtime: approximately 10% attrition rate.
- Overtime is one of the strongest single risk factors, causing 3 times higher attrition.

Department:

Job Level:

Job Satisfaction:

Attrition by Experience and Tenure:

5. MACHINE LEARNING MODEL
======================================================

Algorithm: Gradient Boosting Classifier (scikit-learn)
Class Balancing: SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Oversampling Technique) to handle the 84/16 class imbalance
Training Approach: Standard train-test split with hyperparameter tuning

Performance Metrics on Test Set:
- Accuracy: 86%
- Recall for Attrition class (Yes): 41%
- Precision for Attrition class (Yes): 47%
- F1 Score: 0.44
- ROC-AUC: 0.80

Model Performance Interpretation:
- Overall accuracy of 86% indicates strong general classification ability.
- ROC-AUC of 0.80 demonstrates strong discriminative power between attrition and non-attrition cases.
- Recall of 41% means the model identifies about 4 out of every 10 employees who actually leave. This is reasonable given the difficulty of predicting human behavior.
- Precision of 47% means roughly half the employees flagged as high-risk will actually leave.
- The precision-recall tradeoff reflects the inherent challenge of predicting rare events in imbalanced datasets.
- SMOTE improved minority class detection compared to a baseline model without oversampling.

Why Gradient Boosting was chosen:
- Handles non-linear relationships between features effectively.
- Robust to outliers in the data.
- Provides built-in feature importance rankings.
- Works well with mixed feature types including both numerical and categorical variables.
- Ensemble approach (combining many weak learners) reduces overfitting risk.

7. BUSINESS RECOMMENDATIONS
======================================================

Immediate High-Impact Actions:

1. Stock Option Program Expansion:
   Extend stock options to more employees, especially entry-level and sales roles. This is the number one predictor — employees with zero stock options show 25%+ attrition. Even Level 1 options cut attrition roughly in half.

2. Overtime Management:
   Implement strict overtime policies and monitoring. Overtime workers have 3 times higher attrition (30% vs 10%). Consider hiring additional staff to distribute workload, or introduce mandatory rest periods.

3. Competitive Compensation Review:
   Conduct market salary benchmarking for employees earning below $3,000 per month. Low income is a primary attrition driver. Targeted raises for the bottom quartile can yield significant retention improvements.

Strategic Medium-Term Actions:

4. Sales Department Retention Program:
   Sales Representatives have 40% attrition — the highest of any role. Review sales targets, commission structures, career progression paths, and work-life balance for this group specifically.

5. Early Career Engagement:
   Focus retention programs on employees within their first 2 years. Implement structured onboarding, mentoring programs, and clear career development plans. The first 24 months are the highest-risk period.

6. Work-Life Balance Initiatives:
   Introduce flexible working arrangements, remote work options, and wellness programs. Poor work-life balance causes 31% attrition — this is actionable and preventable.

7. Job Satisfaction Improvement:
   Conduct regular satisfaction surveys, establish feedback mechanisms, and create action plans for departments or teams with low satisfaction scores. Improve recognition and reward systems.

Ongoing Monitoring Actions:

8. Predictive Risk Monitoring:
   Use this system's prediction module to regularly score all employees and identify high-risk individuals before they resign. Proactive intervention is far more effective than reactive measures.

9. Stay Interviews:
   Conduct proactive conversations with employees flagged as high-risk by the model. Understand their specific concerns and address them individually.

10. Travel Policy Review:
    Reassess frequent travel requirements, as they contribute to 25% attrition. Offer alternatives like video conferencing or compensatory benefits for frequent travelers.

11. Commute Support:
    Provide transportation benefits, subsidize relocation, or offer remote work options for employees with commutes exceeding 20 miles.
